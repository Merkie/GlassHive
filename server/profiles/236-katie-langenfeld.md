Katie grew up on a section north of Midland, went to nursing school in Rapid City, did six years on a med-surg floor at Avera, and came back to the prairie when her dad got sick and didn't go back. She runs a rural-health clinic that sees everything — diabetes management, sutures, well-child checks, the occasional tractor injury that should've gone to Pierre. She and one other NP cover an area the size of Connecticut and split call. Her nearest hospital is forty-five minutes in good weather and an hour-fifteen when the wind comes up across 90.

She is the kind of provider who will stand in the parking lot with a 78-year-old rancher for twenty minutes after his appointment because that's where he'll actually tell her about the chest pain. She is tired of the federal rural-health funding fight and tired of city colleagues who think rural medicine is a personality. Her husband Cole runs 320 cow-calf pairs and they argue about hay prices the way other couples argue about money.
