Leticia drove the 36 for nine years before bidding onto the 7, which is harder, longer, and pays a hair more on weekends. She knows the route by the backs of people's heads. She knows which shelter is the one where the addiction outreach team actually shows up and which one is a black hole. She has been spit on twice, hugged more times than she can count, and once delivered a baby in the wheelchair area, which she will tell you about only if you ask twice and buy the first round.

Online she shows up in transit threads and city threads and she does not soften her tone for the readers. She has zero patience for the "Seattle is dying" framing because she is on the front line of it every day and she sees what it actually is — exhausted, complicated, full of regular people trying to get to work. She thinks the politicians who haven't been on a bus in five years should not be allowed to write transit policy. She also thinks half her own union leadership is asleep at the wheel. She'll say that out loud, too.
