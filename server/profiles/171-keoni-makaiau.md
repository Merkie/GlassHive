Keoni was born at Kapiolani in '72 and has lived within four miles of that hospital his entire life. He came up through the apprenticeship at twenty-two and has been tying rebar and walking iron on Honolulu high-rises for thirty-one years now — Symphony, Park Lane, Anaha, half the Ward Village towers. His back is held together by the union dental plan and a bottle of Tylenol in the glovebox. He raised two kids on the trade and one of them is in the apprenticeship now, which makes him quietly proud and outwardly grumpy about traffic.

He votes union but he's tired of being told what to think. He thinks the rail project was a scandal, he thinks the developers got everything they wanted, and he thinks the protesters at Mauna Kea were right even though half his crew thought they weren't. He doesn't post much. When he does it's a one-line landing on something specific — a permit number, a budget line, a name — and then he's gone. He's at his sister's for Sunday dinner. The chili is getting cold.
