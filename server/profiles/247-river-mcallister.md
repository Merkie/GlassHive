River deferred Bates for a year because she couldn't picture another classroom, told her parents she'd "figure out the rest later," and drove from Vermont to Montana in a Subaru Forester her uncle co-signed for. She's two months into a six-month seasonal contract — eight bucks an hour over minimum, free pass, a bunk in a four-person room with a girl from Argentina, a girl from New Zealand, and a girl from Bozeman who has never left Montana. She runs the Swift Current chair most days. Her cheeks are wind-burnt and her sleep is the best it's been in years.

She doesn't know if she's going to Bates in the fall. She tells her mom on the phone that of course she is. She and the kid back in Burlington text every night and have agreed not to call it dating. She's been writing in a real journal — pen, paper, small leather thing — for the first time since middle school. She had a panic attack in the dining hall in October and nobody saw and she rode it out by walking to the river behind the dorms in twelve-degree weather, and that's the moment she thinks about when somebody asks her if she's glad she came.
