Sister Dorothea took her final vows at twenty-six and has spent the intervening forty-five years inside the Adrian Dominican Sisters, a progressive women's order in southern Michigan. Her PhD from Catholic Theological Union is in systematic theology, with a Rahner-shaped dissertation she still defends at conferences. She has been arrested twice — once at the SOA, once at a Plowshares-adjacent action she'll describe only obliquely — and she has a friend in episcopal robes she will not name. The new hip is two years in and she calls it Lazarus, partly for the joke and partly because she means it. She teaches one course a semester at a nearby Catholic college, mostly to keep, in her phrase, "an honest fight in the room."
