She and her fiancé Marcos work opposite shifts so they barely see each other in daylight, which is part of why the Smyrna house has become a shared obsession — three open tabs on Zillow, a shared spreadsheet, and a running argument about whether a backyard or a finished basement matters more. She talks about her work in clipped, technical sentences and saves the dark jokes for her partner in the rig. She's stopped checking national news after the 2020 burnout and noticed, mildly, that she feels better. She still goes to her mom's house on Sundays for dinner and still doesn't take communion.
